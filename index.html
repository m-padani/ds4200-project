MBTA Transit Inequality

Analyzing prediction reliability and rider demographics across MBTA transit modes and subway lines.

Project framing

Project Overview

Research Question

This project asks whether MBTA prediction accuracy is distributed evenly across transit services and whether differences in service reliability overlap with differences in rider demographics. More specifically, it examines whether unreliable service information may contribute to unequal rider experiences across Boston’s transit system.

The analysis focuses on two connected questions. First, how does reliability vary across transit modes and subway lines over time? Second, do those operational differences overlap with patterns of transit dependence, income, race, and household vehicle access?

Data Sources

Two datasets support the analysis. The first is MBTA rapid transit and bus prediction accuracy data, which includes weekly prediction counts, accurate predictions, service mode, and route identifiers. The second is the MBTA 2024 System-Wide Passenger Survey, which includes weighted demographic measures such as low-income status, minority status, race, and vehicle access across MBTA service modes and rapid transit groupings.

After cleaning, the data was used to compute accuracy rate, error rate, rolling accuracy, and route-level summaries. These datasets are well suited to the project because they allow service quality and rider composition to be examined together. One limitation is that the survey data is aggregated, so it cannot capture every rider-level difference within a line or neighborhood.

Visualization 1

Prediction Accuracy Over Time

Interpretation

This time series shows a persistent reliability gap between bus and subway predictions across the 2020 to 2026 period. Bus predictions remain consistently stronger, usually staying in the mid-80% range, while subway accuracy falls more often into the upper-70% to low-80% range. The brush interaction helps isolate local patterns such as short-term dips, seasonal shifts, and the broader widening gap after 2023.

In equity terms, this matters because inaccurate arrival information affects how riders plan commutes, transfers, and arrival times for work or school. A sustained gap means that prediction quality is not evenly distributed across the system. Reliability here is not just a technical metric. It shapes whether transit information can be trusted as a daily planning tool.

Visualization 2

Prediction Accuracy by Time Horizon

Interpretation

This chart compares prediction accuracy across forecast windows for bus and subway service. The pattern matters because overall averages can hide whether systems perform differently when riders are planning in advance versus checking real-time arrivals. Hovering reveals the exact values for each bar.

The visual suggests that subway prediction quality weakens more noticeably at longer horizons, while bus predictions remain comparatively steadier. That means riders relying on subway information to make forward-looking travel decisions may face greater uncertainty. In practical terms, weaker long-horizon predictions can translate into less confidence when deciding whether to leave early, wait, transfer, or seek another option.

Visualization 3

Rider Demographics by Transit Mode

Interpretation

This view compares low-income share, minority share, and zero-vehicle households across MBTA modes. The dropdown allows the same layout to be reused across measures, making it easier to compare how the demographic ranking of modes shifts or remains stable.

Bus riders stand out as the most transit-dependent group across all three indicators, while ferry riders sit at the opposite end. That pattern is essential to the project’s equity framing. It shows that the system does not serve one uniform public. Different modes serve different populations, and those differences matter when reliability and information quality are unevenly distributed.

Visualization 4

Subway Accuracy vs. Minority Ridership

Interpretation

This scatter plot directly tests whether line-level prediction quality aligns with differences in minority ridership. Each point represents a subway line, while point size shows total prediction volume. Hovering reveals line name, prediction accuracy, minority ridership, low-income ridership, and total predictions together.

The pattern is not perfectly linear, which suggests that operational factors such as infrastructure, service complexity, or line conditions also influence reliability. Still, the chart makes clear that accuracy is not evenly distributed across lines that serve different rider populations. That matters because inequity can emerge not only between modes, but also within the subway network itself.

Visualization 5

Racial Composition by Transit Mode

Interpretation

The stacked bar chart shows that rider composition varies sharply by mode. Bus riders are the most racially diverse, while ferry riders are the least diverse. The click-to-highlight interaction allows one category to be isolated across all modes without removing the broader distribution.

This matters because service quality should be evaluated alongside who is being served. Even when the MBTA’s overall performance metrics appear acceptable, the lived impact can still differ substantially if the parts of the system used by different groups do not perform equally well. This chart supports the broader claim that transit reliability has an equity dimension, not just an operational one.

Visualization 6

Subway Line Accuracy Over Time

Interpretation

This final visualization shows that reliability varies not only by mode, but also across subway lines over time. Some lines remain comparatively stable, while others experience repeated drops and wider swings in performance. The legend toggle helps reduce clutter, and the hover crosshair allows closer comparison at specific dates.

The significance of this chart is that it shifts the equity question from broad averages to the rider’s actual line-level experience. If some lines consistently underperform, then the quality of transit information is geographically uneven. Riders on those lines face a different system than riders on stronger-performing lines, even if both are formally part of the same network.

Conclusion

Summary of Findings and Future Work

The visualizations show that prediction accuracy varies across transit modes and routes, with bus systems generally more consistent and some subway lines, such as Green Line E, performing worse. These differences suggest that reliability is uneven and may affect rider experience across communities. The demographic analysis also indicates that more vulnerable populations rely more on certain transit modes, raising potential equity concerns.

In the future, this work could be improved by using more detailed data, such as neighborhood-level information, and incorporating external factors like weather or service disruptions to better explain changes in accuracy.

Analysis

Our analysis examines how transit prediction reliability varies across modes, routes, and time, and how these differences may contribute to infrastructure inequality in Boston. Using the cleaned dataset, we created additional features such as accuracy rate, error rate, rolling accuracy, and route-level performance categories to better understand reliability patterns.

We first compared prediction accuracy across transit modes and found that bus predictions were generally more consistent than subway predictions in the updated visualizations. This suggests that riders across different parts of the system do not receive the same level of reliability in transit information.

We then analyzed trends over time, which showed that reliability changes across weeks, across forecast horizons, and across subway lines. Some lines remained relatively stable, while others showed repeated drops and wider swings in performance.

At the demographic level, the visualizations showed that bus riders are more likely to be low-income, minority, and from zero-vehicle households. This means the groups that depend most on transit are often using the parts of the system where information quality and reliability may be less even.

Overall, our findings show that transit reliability is unevenly distributed across the MBTA system, and that this unevenness overlaps with differences in who relies on each mode. This supports the idea that transit prediction quality is not only an operational issue, but also an equity issue.
